Banking Monogamy Is Dead

Loyalty in banking has not disappeared.
It has fragmented.

Across recent Innovatr financial services studies, over 60% of urban consumers report actively using more than one financial provider. Not considering. Not trialling. Using.

Primary bank for salary.
Different app for payments.
Separate provider for credit.
Another platform for savings.

The idea that one bank owns the relationship is eroding quietly.

What we see in data is not anger. It is pragmatism.

Interest in new financial propositions remains strong. In multiple recent concept screens, more than 70% of respondents indicate initial appeal for simplified digital banking tools. But when we track commitment to switching, the number drops below 40% in many cases.

That gap tells the story.

Consumers are open. They are not devoted.

In South Africa, this fragmentation is amplified by lived reality. Income streams are often layered. Gig income. Informal transfers. Family support. A single platform rarely fits every need. So consumers build financial portfolios the way they build investment portfolios. Diversified. Functional. Flexible.

Where banks misread this shift is assuming that strong NPS equals security. It does not. Satisfaction does not prevent parallel adoption.

Winning in this environment requires modular thinking. Instead of fighting for exclusivity, brands need to win specific use cases. Payments. Travel. Youth accounts. SME lending. Micro savings. Each product must stand alone commercially.

Innovatr POV
Banking loyalty is no longer binary. It is situational. The brands that win will not ask to be everything. They will dominate something.

If your portfolio assumes customer permanence, it is time to pressure test that assumption properly. Commitment modelling reveals far more than satisfaction scores ever will.

The New Non Negotiable Treat

Conventional logic says indulgence falls when budgets tighten.
Our data says the opposite.

In a recent Innovatr FMCG survey, 80% of South Africans agreed with the statement:
“I always make room in my budget for a small treat, even when money is tight.”

More telling, 72% agreed that “small daily indulgences feel more important now than they did two years ago.”

This is not reckless spending. It is emotional budgeting.

When we segment behaviour by income band, the pattern holds. Even lower income households report protecting at least one small, regular comfort purchase. The size of the treat changes. The ritual does not.

What has shifted is how indulgence is justified.

Over 60% of respondents say they are more selective about when and how they indulge compared to three years ago. That means fewer big splurges. More controlled moments. Smaller pack formats. Familiar brands.

Across concept testing, premium snack ideas with strong nostalgia cues outperform experimental flavour launches by as much as 12 points in commitment. Novelty creates interest. Familiarity drives repeat.

In South Africa, this behaviour makes sense. Financial pressure is constant. Energy instability adds stress. Daily life feels heavier. Small predictable comforts provide control.

Where brands get this wrong is by confusing indulgence with extravagance. Consumers are not chasing luxury. They are protecting relief.

Innovatr POV
Treats have moved from optional to protective. Brands that design for emotional affordability will outperform those designing for spectacle.

Affordable Indulgence

Small luxuries are becoming strategic purchases.

Across Innovatr category work, we see a consistent pattern. When asked where they would cut back first, consumers point to big ticket lifestyle upgrades. Eating out. Fashion splurges. Subscription stacking.

But when asked what they refuse to remove entirely, the answers shift.

In recent research, 67% of South Africans agreed with the statement:
“I would rather reduce a large expense than give up all small pleasures.”

That is the affordable indulgence paradox.

Economic pressure has not killed indulgence. It has refined it.

In beverage testing, smaller premium formats outperform larger value packs on repeat intent when price difference remains within a psychologically manageable range. Consumers accept paying slightly more per unit when the total spend feels contained.

In beauty, entry premium formats with clear functional reassurance see commitment rates up to 15 points higher than transformation driven premium products requiring long routines.

The logic is consistent.

Consumers are not trading down. They are trading strategically.

In South Africa, where income volatility and cost inflation coexist, this behaviour is rational. Big risks feel irresponsible. Small pleasures feel stabilising.

The commercial implication is important. Brands that can deliver moments of genuine emotional payoff within controlled price bands win both frequency and loyalty.

Where teams often misstep is assuming that affordability means cheap positioning. It does not. It means contained commitment.

Innovatr POV
Affordable indulgence is not about lowering price. It is about lowering risk. The brands that master this balance will grow even in constrained markets.
